NYC Public Housing & Hurricane Risk

When Evacuation
Is Uneven

Mapping hurricane risk and evacuation access gaps
for NYCHA developments in New York City

By Allison Han  ·  NYU Web Mapping · 2026

↓  Scroll to explore

"In New York City, hurricane risk is not only about where water reaches — it is also about who can realistically get to safety."

When hurricane risk is mapped in New York City, the focus usually falls on the coastline, flood zones, or evacuation areas. But for residents of public housing, another question matters just as much: when a storm is approaching, how easy is it to reach a designated evacuation center?

NYCHA, the New York City Housing Authority, operates hundreds of developments across all five boroughs, housing hundreds of thousands of residents who may face additional challenges during evacuation, older adults, people with disabilities, and households with fewer transportation options.

This project asks: which NYCHA developments face the greatest evacuation disadvantage during hurricanes — and what happens when transit access is factored in?

Step 1 of 5

NYCHA developments are deeply tied to the city's hurricane risk geography

The first step is to place NYCHA developments in the context of New York City's five boroughs. Public housing is not evenly distributed — a noticeable share sits in coastal or low-lying parts of the city, especially along southern Brooklyn, parts of Queens, Lower Manhattan, and Staten Island.

This matters because it shows that hurricane preparedness for public housing is not a niche problem. It is woven into the geography of coastal exposure itself.

Step 2 of 5

Exposure does not automatically mean access

Designated hurricane evacuation centers were mapped with a 0.5-mile buffer as a proxy for walking proximity. NYCHA developments falling outside that buffer represent an access gap, places where the burden of reaching safety falls more heavily on residents themselves.

The pattern shows that evacuation infrastructure is not evenly aligned with where vulnerable housing is located.

Step 3 of 5

The access gap is sharpest along the coast

Zooming into South Brooklyn reveals the spatial logic of the gap: developments closest to the water are often the farthest from official evacuation infrastructure.

In places like Coney Island and Red Hook, coastal exposure and distance from evacuation centers converge — creating the highest concentration of evacuation disadvantage in the city.

Step 4 of 5

From spatial pattern to planning priority

The analysis points to four geographic clusters where evacuation planning investment is most urgently needed: the South Bronx, southern Brooklyn, southeast Queens, and Staten Island's North Shore.

These are places where NYCHA density, coastal risk, and access gaps converge, and where targeted outreach, shelter capacity, and transportation planning could make the greatest difference.

Step 5 of 5

When distance compounds distance

For many NYCHA residents, the challenge is not just one gap — it is two gaps stacked together. Some developments are located both outside the 0.5-mile reach of an evacuation center and outside the 0.25-mile walkable range of a subway station.

During a hurricane, public transit is typically suspended before landfall. For residents without a car, walking becomes the only realistic option. These doubly isolated developments face the steepest evacuation burden in the entire city.

How the analysis was built

🗄

Data Sources

Three public datasets from NYC Open Data: NYCHA Public Housing Developments, Hurricane Evacuation Centers, and Subway Stations. Representative samples are embedded in the code for live computation.

⭕

Buffer Analysis

A 0.5-mile buffer was generated around each evacuation center using Turf.js, accounting for real geographic distance via Haversine calculation. A 0.25-mile subway walkshed buffer was applied using the same method.

🗺

QGIS Spatial Analysis

The complete spatial workflow — reprojection to EPSG:2263 (NY State Plane), buffer generation, Difference and Intersection tools — was performed in QGIS on the full citywide NYCHA dataset, identifying 132 doubly isolated developments.

🔴

Double Isolation

Developments were flagged as doubly isolated if they fell outside both the evacuation center buffer and the subway walkshed — representing the most acute evacuation disadvantage in the dataset. Results were verified against the full QGIS analysis.
